Notes on learning French

Like many people, I was taught the basics of French in high school and then later found that I still had a lot to learn as my needs became more demanding: from passing exams in a non-francophone country, to basic interactions in France, to working, socialising, and studying often completely in French. My French is much improved, though I am still frustrated that I do not always understand completely, and that I cannot always express myself as adequately as I would like. Since people sometimes ask me for pointers on learning French, I have written these notes on the ideas and resources that I find most useful. These notes are

  • subjective—they are about what worked for me, but they will not be optimal for everybody;
  • for intermediate learners—I was taught the basics of French by competent teachers, these notes are about how I worked, and continue to work, to improve myself;
  • not rigorous—I have no formal training in language teaching, I did not keep references for the meta-theory, and I give opinions based on a single data point (me!); and
  • sometimes anglophone specific—several of the resources are for English speakers learning French.

The approach I describe is based on spaced repetition, mostly online resources, one or two technical books, and the principles: (a) theory guides but never replaces practice, and (b) the skills of listening, reading, speaking, and writing required separate but coordinated efforts. The approach aims at eventual mastery of the language, that is, for precision, range, and nuance in comprehension and production, rather than for minimum working knowledge. Objections and suggestions are always welcome.

The first six sections of the notes reflect the structure I chose for my studies. The last section reflects on what I wished I had avoided or done differently.

Vocabulary and spaced repetition

Every language student must learn to associate thousands of words—essentially arbitrary graphical and phonetic forms—with their meanings. Spaced repetition is a powerful technique for memorising thousands of words efficiently. Basically, you start by creating or downloading a set of virtual ‘flashcards’ for the vocabulary that you want to learn. Cards have two sides: one for the foreign word to learn, and the other for its meaning(s), usually in your native language. You then regularly quiz yourself with a computer program that successively presents you with one side of a card, and you try to recall the other side before it is shown to you. Upon seeing the correct answer, you grade your performance in a range from ‘completely forgotten’ to ‘too easy’. The program uses your responses to decide when next to quiz you on each given card: those you forget are shown more and more often, and those you remember are shown less and less often.

The basic idea is simple and it works remarkably well because

  1. it is based on studies of human memory (the spacing effect);
  2. the daily reviews quickly become a habit and ensure that you spend some time revising each day;
  3. the algorithm is very efficient, you can have a database of tens of thousands of cards but review only tens each day (for instance, I have amassed 24 000 cards over seven years and I need only review between 100 and 150 a day).

This Wired article is a very nice introduction to spaced repetition and the work and ideas of Piotr Wozniak who developed the modern, computer-based SuperMemo algorithm—compared to the earlier Pimsleur graduated interval and Leitner systems. Several spaced repetition programs now exist. The main ones are Mnemosyne and Anki. Both are free software. I use Mnemosyne but Anki is more popular. Both applications have the following essential characteristics:

  1. efficient editing of flashcards with colours, fonts, images, sounds, and special characters (like those of the IPA),
  2. a simple interface that helps you focus on real learning and avoid procrastinating on a computer, and
  3. simple and solid support for reviewing cards on a mobile phone (gimmicky animations and a multitude of buttons and menus may seem fun at first, but ultimately become obstacles to, and distractions from efficient learning).

Support for mobile review is almost a critical feature. It allows you to employ time spent on public transport or waiting in queues to do your daily language study. Since the study is already broken into small pieces (individual cards), it does not matter that your study is interrupted or spread out over several sessions; it may even be beneficial. I developed Mnemojojo and Mnemododo for reviewing Mnemosyne cards on mobile phones. Many people seem happy with AnkiDroid and AnkiMobile.

Choosing vocabulary

Spaced repetition is a powerful tool for organizing and learning words, but which words should you learn? The question is an important one since there are potentially very many words to learn and most of us have limited capacity and time. One approach would be to create or download cards for the 1000 to 3000 most frequently used nouns and verbs. This ideas seems rational,1 but I did not try it, since I already had a rudimentary vocabulary, so I cannot comment on how well it works. Instead, the approach I take is to systematically note and add words as I come across them when reading or listening, or when I fail to express myself for lack of the right word. For instance, I underline words in newspaper articles, make lists as I read novels or listen to people speaking, and regularly search for translations of the English words, expressions, and constructions that I need. There are two main advantages to creating a list manually in this way, as opposed to downloading someone else's ready-made list. First, the resulting vocabulary is aligned with one's interests, needs, and milieu. Second, by copying words onto lists, looking them up online, and then adapting material into a flashcard, you are already paying attention to the words and familiarising yourself with their shapes and meanings. In your own private lists you can copy-and-paste definitions and examples directly from dictionaries, and use images and sounds from websites, without having to worry about crediting the authors or respecting copyright.
